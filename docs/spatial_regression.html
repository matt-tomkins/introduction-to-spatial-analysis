7 Spatial Regression

7.1 Introduction

Aims

The aims of this practical are to:

  1. Understand how spatial regression is different from classical (aspatial) regression and why it matters.
  2. Understand the differences between common forms of spatial regression.
  3. Use spatial regression to reveal relationships in a sociological context.

Application

To that end, we’ll apply aspatial and spatial regression to predict life expectancy at the MSOA-level (Middle Layer Super Output Area) in Greater Manchester.

Data

The data for this practical are sourced from the Department of Health and Social Care (DHSC, Fingertips) and the Office for National Statistics (ONS), and are available in [data/dhsc] and [data/ons/msoa] respectively. Key attributes have been retained and are described below:

  • ONS MSOA-level geometries and lookup table [Source]
  • ONS Economic Activity Status [Source]:
    • employment_dna: Does not apply
    • employed_not_ftstudent: Economically active (excluding full-time students): In employment
    • employed_and_ftstudent: Economically active and a full-time student: In employment
    • economically_inactive_not_ftstudent: Economically inactive (excluding full-time students)
    • economically_inactive_and_ftstudent: Economically inactive and a full-time student
    • economic_status_total: The total number of individuals in the corresponding geographical area with an economic activity status. Note some peripheral classes have been removed to simplify the analysis, so the total is not the sum of the attributes above.
  • ONS Highest level of qualification [Source]:
    • qualification_dna: Does not apply
    • no_qualifications: No qualifications
    • level_one: Level 1 and entry level qualifications e.g., 1 to 4 GCSEs grade A* to C
    • level_two: Level 2 qualifications e.g., 5 or more GCSEs (A* to C or 9 to 4)
    • level_three: Level 3 qualifications e.g., 2 or more A levels or VCEs
    • level_four: Level 4 qualifications or above e.g., degree (BA, BSc) or higher degree (MA, PhD, PGCE)
    • apprenticeships_or_similar: Apprenticeships, vocational or work-related qualifications
    • qualification_total: The total number of individuals in the corresponding geographical area with a qualification status
  • ONS Disability [Source]:
  • disabled: Disabled under the Equality Act
  • not_disabled: Not disabled under the Equality Act
  • disabled_perc: The percentage of individuals classed as disabled under the Equality Act, rounded to two decimal places
  • ONS Household income [Source]
    • total_annual_income: Total annual income (£)
  • DHSC Index of Multiple Deprivation [Source]:
    • imd_decile: Index of Multiple Deprivation
  • DHSC Fuel poverty [Source]:
    • fuel_poverty: the percentage of households in the corresponding geographical area that were in fuel poverty, rounded to two decimal places.
    • household_poverty: the number of households (modelled) in fuel poverty
    • household_total: the total number of households
  • DHSC Life Expectancy [Source]:
    • life_expectancy: Life expectancy at birth
    • CI_lower: Lower 95% confidence interval
    • CI_uppwe: Upper 95% confidence interval

7.2.4 Spatial Regression

The spatial distribution of residuals indicates that spatial autocorrelation is present. We should therefore consider using a spatial regression method, documented well here.

To do discuss why it is referred to as autoregression i.e., accounting for self-correlation. In a time-series setting, this is a method that predicts future values based on past values of the same variable. In our context, we are predicting values based on neighbouring values for the feature of interest.

Spatial error model

Broadly, the spatial error model (SEM) treats residual autocorrelation as a nuisance, and handles it as if the spatial clustering was an accident, see here and summarised well by Darmofal (2015), cited by Solymosi, R., and Medina, J. :

“spatial dependence observed in our data does not reflect a truly spatial process, but merely the geographical clustering of the sources of the behaviour of interest. For example, citizens in adjoining neighbourhoods may favour the same (political) candidate not because they talk to their neighbors, but because citizens with similar incomes tend to cluster geographically, and income also predicts vote choice. Such spatial dependence can be termed attributional dependence. For example, citizens in adjoining neighbourhoods may favour the same (political) candidate not because they talk to their neighbors, but because citizens with similar incomes tend to cluster geographically, and income also predicts vote choice. Such spatial dependence can be termed attributional dependence.”

Spatial lag model

Unlike the spatial error model, which sees spatial autocorrelation of residuals as a “nuisance”, the spatial lag model assumes spatial dependence in the outcome variable (life_expectancy). This is addressed by adding an independent variable to the model that is the average value of the neighbors for each observation i.e., each observation is partly dependent on its neighbours values. This is referred to as the spatially lagged variable.

According to Darmofal (2015), cited by Solymosi, R., and Medina, J., spatial autocorrelation can be produced by diffusion of behaviour between neighbouring units:

“If so the behaviour is likely to be highly social in nature, and understanding the interactions between interdependent units is critical to understanding the behaviour in question. For example, citizens may discuss politics across adjoining neighbours such that an increase in support for a candidate in one neighbourhood directly leads to an increase in support for the candidate in adjoining neighbourhoods”

In this situation, a spatial lag model would be most appropriate.

7.2.4.1 Model selection: Lagrange Multiplier

The result above for the Spatial Lag Model indicates that some spatial structure remains unexplained or there is model misspecification.

In this situation, our preference would be for the spatial error model, which has done a good job of accounting for spatial autocorrelation. We could also choose the model based on theoretical grounds, or information about the phenomenon we are observing.

We have other tools at our disposal to select the best model though, one of which is the Lagrange Multiplier, developed by Bera and Yoon (1993), and applied to spatial regression by Anselin et al. (1996), with the process outlined well in the ESRI documentation.

The process, as described in detail by Anselin and Rey (2014), involves calculating the following LM tests:

  • \(LM_{lag}\): Tests for omitted spatially lagged dependent variables.
  • \(LM_{error}\): Tests for spatial autocorrelation in the residuals.
  • \(\mathrm{Robust}\ LM_{lag}\): Tests for lag dependence in the presence of potential error dependence.
  • \(\mathrm{Robust}\ LM_{error}\): Tests for error dependence in the presence of potential lag dependence.

Based on the results of these tests:

  • If both \(LM_{lag}\) and \(LM_{error}\) are not significant, then we can simply use OLS.
  • If only one is significant, that is the model of choice.
  • If both are significant, then calculate more robust test statistics as described above (\(\mathrm{Robust}\ LM_{lag}\), \(\mathrm{Robust}\ LM_{error}\)) and repeat the process. If only one of the robust tests is significant, that is the model of choice.
